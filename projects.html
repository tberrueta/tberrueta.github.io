Projects

Control of Complex Systems

Complex systems across artificial and natural contexts routiniely exhibit emergent behaviors that challenge microscopic mechanistic understanding. In collaboration with the groups of Drs. Daniel Goldman, Kurt Wiesenfeld, and Jeremy England, I have been working towards developing theories of self-organization for nonequilibrium dynamical systems. Through these theories of spontenous order, I am constructing novel control frameworks for steering emergent behavior in complex systems towards desired outcomes.

Colloidal Microrobotics

Microrobots promise to revolutionize the fields of biotechnology and healthcare by enabling dextrous non-invasive medical procedures at the micro-scale. While progress has been made in the development of independent actuators, sensors, and batteries, producing fully-integrated autonomous microrobotic systems has proven to be a much greater challenge. Due to power and footprint constraints, deploying robots at this scale requires components that play multiple roles and exploit their material properties to achieve greater functionality. To this end, I have been collaborating with the group of Dr. Michael Strano at MIT to develop more capable untethered microscopic robots. By exploiting the physics of self-orgranization, material properties, and novel control frameworks, I am working towards the development of fully untethered microrobots.

Information-Theoretic Analysis of Human Motion

In order to develop effective assistive robotic therapies, the information-theoretic qualities of human motion need to be better understood. There are many challenges that hinder progress in this area, such as the potential high-dimensionality, nonlinearity, and nonsmoothness of human movements. These challenges demand algorithmic techniques that mitigate these issues. To this end, I have been working on the development of efficient data-driven techniques that enable low-dimensional quantitative modeling of human movement.
