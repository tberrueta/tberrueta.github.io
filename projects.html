Projects

Controlling Emergent Behavior in Complex Systems

Complex systems across artificial and natural contexts routiniely exhibit emergent behaviors that challenge microscopic mechanistic understanding. In collaboration with the groups of Drs. Daniel Goldman, Kurt Wiesenfeld, and Jeremy England, I have been working towards developing theories of self-organization for nonequilibrium dynamical systems. Through these novel theories of spontenous order, I am constructing novel control frameworks for exploiting emergent behavior in active collectives towards desired outcomes.

Information-Theoretic Analysis of Human Motion

In order to develop effective assistive robotic therapies, the information-theoretic qualities of human motion needs to be better understood. There are many challenges that hinder progress in this area, such as the potential high-dimensionality, nonlinearity, and nonsmoothness of human movements. These challenges demand algorithmic techniques that mitgate these issues. To this end, I have been working on the development of efficient data-driven techniques that enable low-dimensional quantitative modeling of human movement.
